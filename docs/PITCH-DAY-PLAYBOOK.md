## 3-Minute Pitch Structure

You have 180 seconds. Every section is timed. This is not the video — it's tighter, more direct, and you're talking TO the judges, not at a camera.

### Opening — Shashwati (30 seconds)
**Hook with Sarah's story, compressed.**
> "This is Sarah. She's 34, partially sighted, lives in Liverpool. Last week she tried to order flowers online. The checkout button had no label. Her screen reader couldn't find it. She tried three times, then closed the tab. The florist lost a sale and doesn't even know why."

*Why Shashwati:* She owns the customer narrative and the empathy angle.

### Problem + Market — Shashwati (30 seconds)
**Three stats, no more. Land them hard.**
- 94.8% of websites fail accessibility standards
- 16 million disabled people in the UK — £274 billion spending power
- Equality Act 2010 makes this a legal obligation, not a nice-to-have

*Transition line:* "Most small businesses have no idea. That's the gap."

### Solution — Vedant (30 seconds)
**What NeuroEdge does, in plain language.**
> "NeuroEdge is a web tool. You put in a URL, we scan it, and we give you a report in plain English — not developer jargon. What's broken, why it matters, what to fix first. Full audit: £29 to £49. Weekly monitoring: £19 a month."

*Why Vedant:* He built it, he explains it credibly.

### Traction + Validation — Vedant (20 seconds)
> "We've already scanned 30 Liverpool SMEs. Every single one failed. Average score: 38 out of 100. 1,098 errors documented. The demand is there — these businesses just don't have a tool that speaks their language."

### Business Model — Shashwati (20 seconds)
> "Three revenue streams: one-off audits, video walkthroughs at £99, and monitoring subscriptions. 100 subscribers gets us to £22,800 ARR. We're targeting Liverpool first — local networking events, free scans as lead gen, LinkedIn content."

### The Ask — Shashwati (20 seconds)
> "We're asking for £5,000. £500 for user research with disabled testers. £1,000 for marketing. £400 for AI infrastructure. The rest for design, legal, and pilot costs. By month six: 100 paying customers, £5,000 recovered, self-sustaining."

### Close — Vedant (10 seconds)
> "Three weeks after we audit Bloom and Petal, Sarah visits again. This time, the flowers arrive on her mum's birthday. Every audit we sell removes a barrier. That's NeuroEdge."

**Total: ~160 seconds. The remaining 20 seconds is transitions and breathing room.**

## Q&A Preparation — 15 Likely Questions

Agree in advance: Shashwati answers business/market/impact questions. Vedant answers technical/product/feasibility questions.

### Market & Problem
1. **"How do you know SMEs will pay for this?"**
   → We scanned 30 Liverpool businesses. 100% failed. When we showed owners their scores, the reaction was shock — they had no idea. The conversion from "you have a problem" to "fix it for me" is short when the problem is legal and financial.

2. **"Why wouldn't they just use a free tool like WAVE or axe?"**
   → Those tools output developer error codes — "WCAG 2.1 SC 1.1.1 violation." Our reports say "Your checkout button has no label, so screen readers can't find it. Here's how to fix it." SMEs don't have developers. They need plain English.

3. **"Is this really a £274 billion market opportunity for you?"**
   → No, and we're not claiming it is. That's the total spending power of disabled consumers. Our addressable market is UK SMEs willing to pay £29–99 for an audit. We start in Liverpool, prove the model, then expand.

4. **"What if the law isn't enforced?"**
   → The European Accessibility Act came into force June 2025. Enforcement is ramping up. But even without enforcement, 7 in 10 disabled users leave inaccessible sites. This is a revenue problem regardless of legal risk.

### Product & Tech
5. **"What's your technical moat? Can't anyone build this?"**
   → The scanning engines (axe-core, Pa11y) are open source, yes. Our value isn't the scan — it's the AI interpretation layer that turns 50 error codes into a prioritized, plain-English action plan. That interpretation model improves with every audit we run.

6. **"How accurate is the AI interpretation?"**
   → We use Claude's API for the interpretation layer. It doesn't guess — it maps known WCAG violations to plain-English explanations with fix instructions. We validate output against a rubric before any report ships.

7. **"What happens when accessibility standards change?"**
   → The scanning engines are maintained by large open-source communities and update with WCAG revisions. Our interpretation layer is prompt-driven, so updating it for new rules is a configuration change, not a rebuild.

8. **"How long does a scan take?"**
   → Under 60 seconds for a standard SME site. Report generation adds another 2–3 minutes for AI interpretation and PDF formatting.

### Business Model & Financials
9. **"£5,000 seems low. Is that really enough?"**
   → It's enough because we're not building from scratch. The scanning engines are open source. Vedant builds the product. Shashwati runs outreach. We don't need to hire anyone. £5,000 covers infrastructure, research, and marketing to get to 100 customers.

10. **"How do you get to 100 customers in 6 months?"**
    → Free scans at Liverpool networking events create leads. We show a business owner their score, they see failures, we offer the full report. LinkedIn content ("We audited 20 Liverpool businesses — here's what we found") builds credibility. 100 customers from a city of 30,000+ SMEs is conservative.

11. **"What's your customer acquisition cost?"**
    → Near zero initially. Free scans are our acquisition tool — the cost is Shashwati's time at events and the compute cost per scan (pennies). Paid marketing comes later from revenue.

12. **"What if you don't hit 100 customers?"**
    → Even at 30 monitoring subscribers, that's £6,840 ARR plus one-off report revenue. The model works at lower volumes — our costs are low because we're two founders with no salaries and open-source infrastructure.

### Team & Execution
13. **"You're both students. How do you balance this with your degrees?"**
    → Shashwati graduates in 2026, before the heaviest execution phase. Vedant's CS programme runs until 2027, but the technical build is his dissertation-adjacent work. We're not starting from nothing — the prototype, pitch deck, and validation are already done.

14. **"What happens after the funding runs out?"**
    → We're self-sustaining by month 6. The £5,000 is bridge funding to get from prototype to paying customers. Once we have recurring monitoring revenue, the business funds itself.

15. **"Why should we back you two specifically?"**
    → Shashwati's marketing research is literally in disability-inclusive marketing — she's not guessing what the market needs. Vedant built a working product in 12 hours during his Springpod placement and co-founded a digital agency. We've already validated with 30 real businesses. We're not pitching an idea. We're pitching work already done.

## Body Language & Delivery Tips

- **Stand side by side**, not behind a laptop. This is a conversation, not a presentation.
- **Look at the judges**, not at each other or the screen. Make eye contact with each judge at least once.
- **Hand-offs should be seamless.** Don't say "now Vedant will talk about the tech." Just finish your point and let Vedant pick up naturally.
- **Slow down.** Nerves make you speed up. If it feels too slow, it's probably the right pace.
- **Pause after big numbers.** Say "94.8% of websites fail accessibility standards" — then stop for one beat. Let it land.
- **Don't apologize.** Don't say "we're just students" or "it's still early." You've done the work. Own it.
- **If you lose your place**, don't panic. Take a breath. Say the next thing you remember. The judges won't know what you skipped.

## If Things Go Wrong

| Scenario | Response |
|----------|----------|
| Tech fails (slides won't load) | Pitch without slides. You know the numbers. The story carries itself. |
| You go over 3 minutes | Cut the close short. End on the ask: "£5,000. Six months. Self-sustaining." |
| You blank on a number | Round it honestly. "Nearly 95%" instead of "94.8%". Don't freeze. |
| Judge is hostile/skeptical | Stay calm. "That's a fair challenge. Here's how we see it..." |
| Asked something you don't know | "We haven't validated that yet, but here's our assumption and how we'd test it." Never bluff. |
| Other team pitches similar idea | Your differentiator is the work already done — 30 scans, 1,098 errors, a working prototype. Lead with that. |
