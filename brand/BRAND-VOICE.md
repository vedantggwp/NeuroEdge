# Brand Voice Guidelines
## NeuroEdge
### Analysis Date: 2026-03-08

---

## Voice Summary

NeuroEdge speaks like a sharp, knowledgeable friend who happens to be an expert in accessibility law and web technology — but never talks down to you. The voice is confident and direct, grounded in data and plain language, with a moral clarity that makes the business case and the ethical case feel like the same thing. We inform, we translate, we guide — and we never hide behind jargon.

---

## Voice Dimensions

### Formal <-----> Casual: 4/10

Leaning formal but not stiff. We use contractions naturally ("we're", "don't", "can't", "it's") and address the reader directly ("you", "your"). But we maintain a professional gravity — no slang, no emoji in copy, no "hey there!" We're the kind of professional who wears a nice shirt but no tie.

**Evidence:**
- "Most SMEs don't know their websites are inaccessible. They don't know it's a legal obligation. And they don't know what they're losing." — Contractions + direct address, but serious subject matter
- "URL in. Report out. Five steps." — Short, punchy, confident. Not casual — efficient.
- "This isn't a social enterprise. It's a business that makes the web more accessible as a direct byproduct of its revenue model." — Formal structure, casual contractions
- "They're not bad people — they just didn't know." — Empathetic, human, uses contractions
- "We sit in the gap — affordable, honest, readable. Audit and explain." — Stripped-back, declarative

### Serious <-----> Playful: 3/10

Predominantly serious. The subject matter — legal obligations, disability exclusion, revenue loss — demands gravity. But we're not dour. We use storytelling (Sarah's journey), vivid metaphors (flowers, checkout buttons), and rhythm to keep the reader engaged. The playfulness comes through structure and pacing, not jokes.

**Evidence:**
- "She finds a local florist online — Bloom and Petal. Beautiful website. But when her screen reader hits the checkout button… nothing happens." — Narrative tension, not humour
- "This isn't coming. It's here." — Dramatic, urgent, no levity
- "Every pound accounted for." — Confident gravity
- "Doing well by doing good — structurally, not performatively." — A wink of wit, but the point is earnest
- "Every scan makes us smarter" — Optimistic energy, but grounded

### Technical <-----> Simple: 7/10

Firmly on the simple side — this is the core brand promise. We translate technical concepts into plain English. When we do use technical terms (WCAG, axe-core, Pa11y, PSBAR), we always contextualise them or explicitly translate them. We never assume the reader knows what an aria-label is — but we're not afraid to show the technical error code as a "before" to contrast with our plain-English "after."

**Evidence:**
- "Not error codes. Not developer jargon. A report that says: your checkout button is invisible to screen readers." — The entire positioning
- "Error: Missing aria-label on input element [WCAG 2.2 SC 1.3.1]" shown as "What they see today" vs. our translation — Technical used as contrast, not communication
- "axe-core + Pa11y run WCAG 2.2 checks" — Technical when talking to investors/judges, but always contextualised ("powers Google Lighthouse", "used by BBC")
- "UK SMEs with 5-50 employees who've never heard of WCAG" — We define our audience by their non-technical nature

### Reserved <-----> Bold: 7/10

Confident and direct. We make strong claims but back them with data and citations. We name competitors directly. We're transparent about our limitations ("automated tools catch ~30-40% of issues. We're upfront about this"). This radical honesty is itself a bold stance in a market full of overclaiming.

**Evidence:**
- "We're not competing with enterprise tools. We're filling the gap below them." — Direct competitive positioning
- "Accessibility overlay tools (accessiBe, UserWay) are widely criticised by the disability community for not actually fixing underlying issues." — Names names, takes sides
- "We take the honest approach: audit and explain, not mask." — Bold moral stance
- "£5,000 — every pound accounted for" — Confident accountability
- "Competitors can replicate our tech stack. They can't replicate our dataset." — Direct, assertive moat claim

## Brand Personality

- **Primary Archetype: The Guide**
  NeuroEdge's core function is translation — taking something complex and making it understandable. We walk business owners through something they've never encountered. We use clear steps ("Five steps. Enter a URL."), we prioritise fixes by impact, and we offer a personal walkthrough as a premium tier. The entire brand is built around making the unfamiliar navigable.

- **Secondary Archetype: The Rebel (Honest Rebel)**
  We challenge the status quo in two directions: against enterprise tools that price out SMEs, and against overlay widgets that fake accessibility. "Audit and explain, not mask" is a rebel statement. Naming accessiBe/UserWay as controversial, calling out that "virtually no public sector websites were fully compliant" — we tell uncomfortable truths. But it's a principled rebellion, not a brash one.

- **Archetype fit:** Strong. The Guide/Rebel blend is distinctive in the accessibility space, where most players are either neutral technical tools or aggressive enterprise salespeople.

## Vocabulary

### Words We Use

**Action words:**
scan, translate, audit, explain, surface, fix, monitor, flag, unlock, reveal, detect, prioritise

**Descriptive words:**
plain-English, affordable, honest, readable, actionable, branded, automated, understandable, accessible, measurable

**Value words:**
transparent, inclusive, upfront, real, proven, business-framed, human-readable, data-driven

**Industry-specific terms (always contextualised):**
WCAG 2.2, axe-core, Pa11y, Equality Act 2010, purple pound, screen reader, accessibility score, monitoring agent

**Signature phrases:**
- "Plain English, not WCAG jargon"
- "Audit and explain"
- "Revenue at risk"
- "Business impact, not error codes"
- "The gap we fill"
- "Affordable, honest, readable"

### Words We Avoid

| Word/Phrase | Why We Avoid It |
|---|---|
| "Compliance solution" | Too enterprise, too vague |
| "Best-in-class" | Generic marketing filler |
| "Cutting-edge" / "revolutionary" | Overclaiming; our innovation is translation, not technology |
| "Leverage" / "synergy" | Corporate jargon — the opposite of our brand |
| "Seamless" / "frictionless" | Overused, meaningless |
| "Fix your accessibility" | We don't fix sites — we audit and explain |
| "AI-powered" (as primary positioning) | Our AI is the method, not the value. The value is the plain-English report. |
| "Disabled people need help" | Patronising. We frame disability as a market and a right, not a charity case |
| "Simple" (to describe the problem) | The problem isn't simple — our explanation of it is |
| "Game-changer" | Empty hype |
| "Just" / "simply" | Can minimise real complexity customers face |

### Linguistic Patterns

- **Short sentences for impact.** "This isn't coming. It's here." / "Audit and explain." / "Five steps."
- **Em dashes for asides.** "Affordable, human-readable, business-framed — that's the gap we fill."
- **Data-first sentences.** Lead with the number: "94.8% of the top million websites..." / "7 in 10 disabled customers..."
- **Before/after contrasts.** Show the WCAG code, then show our translation.
- **Second person for the customer.** "You get a branded report..." / "Your contact form is invisible..."
- **Named specifics over generics.** "Liverpool SMEs" not "businesses". "Bloom and Petal" not "a florist". "£29" not "affordable pricing."
- **Paragraphs are short.** 1-3 sentences max. White space is a feature.

## Writing Guidelines

### Do's

- **Lead with the human impact, then the data.** Sarah first, then 94.8%.
- **Use contractions.** "We're", "don't", "can't", "it's" — sounds human.
- **Address the reader as "you".** "Your website", "you get", "your contact form."
- **Name specific competitors.** WAVE, axe, Siteimprove, Level Access, accessiBe, UserWay — don't hide behind "other solutions."
- **Show before/after.** WCAG error code vs. our plain-English translation. This is the brand in microcosm.
- **Cite sources.** Every data point gets a citation. This builds credibility with judges and customers alike.
- **Be transparent about limitations.** "Automated tools catch ~30-40% of issues. We're upfront about this." Honesty is a differentiator.
- **Frame disability as a market, not a cause.** "£274 billion spending power" — not "help disabled people."
- **Use active voice.** "We scan your website" not "Your website is scanned by our tool."
- **End with a clear next step.** Every section should point the reader somewhere.

### Don'ts

- **Don't use jargon without translating it.** If you write "WCAG 2.2 AA", add context: "the international accessibility standard."
- **Don't promise to fix websites.** We audit and explain. Remediation is the client's responsibility (for now).
- **Don't use passive voice in CTAs.** "Scan your website" not "Get your website scanned."
- **Don't frame accessibility as charity.** It's a legal requirement and a business opportunity.
- **Don't use more than one exclamation mark per page.**
- **Don't hedge unnecessarily.** "We translate WCAG violations into plain English" — not "We aim to help translate..."
- **Don't use "AI-powered" as the headline.** The AI is the method. "Plain-English accessibility audits" is the value.
- **Don't write paragraphs longer than 3 sentences.** Break them up.
- **Don't use stock photo language.** "Diverse team of professionals" — say what we actually are.
- **Don't talk about disability as tragedy.** "24% of UK people are now disabled" is a fact. Not a sob story.

## Brand Messaging Hierarchy

### Level 1: Tagline (under 10 words)

**"Plain-English accessibility audits for UK small businesses."**

Alternative options:
- "Audit and explain. Not mask."
- "Your website's accessibility, in words you understand."

### Level 2: Value Propositions (1 sentence each)

1. **Translation:** We turn incomprehensible WCAG error codes into plain-English explanations any business owner can understand and act on.
2. **Affordability:** Professional accessibility audits start at £29, not £5,000 — because every SME deserves to know what's broken.
3. **Business framing:** Every report shows revenue at risk and priority fixes ranked by business impact, not technical severity.
4. **Legal clarity:** We surface your obligations under the Equality Act 2010 and the European Accessibility Act before enforcement catches you off guard.
5. **Ongoing monitoring:** Our AI agent scans your site weekly and alerts you to new issues in plain English — no developer required.

### Level 3: Elevator Pitch (30 seconds / ~75 words)

"94.8% of websites fail accessibility standards, and it's a legal obligation under the Equality Act 2010. But SMEs can't understand developer error codes, and professional audits cost £5,000+. NeuroEdge scans any website using axe-core and Pa11y, then uses AI to translate the results into a plain-English business report — showing revenue at risk and priority fixes, not WCAG jargon. Reports start at £29. Ongoing monitoring is £19/month."

### Level 4: Boilerplate (100-150 words)

"NeuroEdge is an AI-powered accessibility audit tool built for UK small and medium-sized businesses. We scan websites for WCAG 2.2 violations using industry-standard engines (axe-core and Pa11y), then translate the results into plain-English business impact reports — showing what's broken, why it matters, which fixes to prioritise, and how much revenue is at risk from inaccessibility. Unlike free developer tools that output unreadable error codes, or enterprise platforms costing £5,000+/year, NeuroEdge delivers affordable, human-readable reports starting at £29. Our monitoring agent tracks accessibility weekly for £19/month. Founded at the University of Liverpool by Shashwati Bhosale (MSc Advanced Marketing) and Vedant Gaikwad (MSc Computer Science), NeuroEdge fills the gap between developer jargon and enterprise pricing — making web accessibility understandable and actionable for the businesses that need it most."

### Level 5: Full Brand Story

**Status:** Partial — the pitch deck narrative (Sarah's story) serves as the emotional brand story. A full origin story should be developed for the website's About page, covering:
- The "aha moment" (scanning 30 Liverpool SMEs and finding zero passing)
- Shashwati's marketing expertise + Vedant's technical execution as complementary founders
- The moral case and the business case as inseparable
- The vision: every UK SME understanding their accessibility posture

## Competitor Voice Comparison

| Dimension | NeuroEdge | WAVE / axe | Siteimprove | accessiBe |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Formal <> Casual | 4/10 | 2/10 | 3/10 | 6/10 |
| Serious <> Playful | 3/10 | 2/10 | 2/10 | 5/10 |
| Technical <> Simple | 7/10 | 2/10 | 4/10 | 8/10 |
| Reserved <> Bold | 7/10 | 3/10 | 4/10 | 8/10 |
| Primary Archetype | Guide | Authority | Authority | Friend |

**Differentiation Assessment:**

- **WAVE/axe** speak almost exclusively to developers in technical language. Their voice is neutral, documentation-style, and assumes expertise. There's no overlap with NeuroEdge's voice territory.

- **Siteimprove** adopts a corporate-enterprise tone — polished, measured, feature-focused. They speak to accessibility teams and compliance officers, not SME owners. Slightly formal, data-rich, but not plain-spoken.

- **accessiBe** is the closest voice competitor — they also target non-technical audiences and use simple language. But their voice is salesy, overclaiming ("instant accessibility"), and they've been publicly criticised for misleading messaging. NeuroEdge's radical honesty ("we catch 30-40% of issues — we're upfront about this") is the polar opposite of accessiBe's approach.

- **Unoccupied territory:** The intersection of plain-English, data-backed honesty, and empathetic storytelling is unclaimed. WAVE is honest but technical. accessiBe is simple but dishonest. Siteimprove is credible but expensive and inaccessible to SMEs. NeuroEdge sits at the intersection of all three values: simple + honest + credible.

## Recommendations

### Immediate Actions
1. **Adopt this document as the brand voice reference** for all copy written from this point forward — website, social media, email sequences, customer reports.
2. **Create a "before/after" bank** — collect 10-15 real WCAG error codes paired with plain-English translations. This is the brand in action and the most powerful marketing asset.
3. **Write the full brand story** (Level 5 in messaging hierarchy) for the website About page before public launch.

### Voice Evolution Opportunities
1. **Develop a "customer report" voice variant** — slightly warmer and more supportive than the pitch voice. The report is received by someone who just learned their website is broken; the tone should be "here's what to do" not "here's what you did wrong."
2. **Create a "thought leadership" voice** for LinkedIn and blog content — same voice, but more analytical and insight-driven. The "UK SME Accessibility Index" (after 500 scans) will be a natural extension.
3. **Plan for Shashwati's voice and Vedant's voice** to have subtle differences in personal content (LinkedIn posts, video narration) while maintaining the same brand voice principles. Shashwati: slightly warmer, marketing-framed. Vedant: slightly more technical, builder-framed.

### Consistency Safeguards
1. **Review all new copy against the Voice Chart** (IS / IS NOT) before publishing.
2. **Run a "jargon check"** on every customer-facing document — if a term isn't in the "Words We Use" list, it needs a plain-English explanation alongside it.
3. **Assign one person as voice owner** (likely Shashwati given her marketing expertise) who approves all external copy for voice consistency.
